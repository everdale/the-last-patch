# The Humor Doctrine

The stakes: humor that falls flat doesn't just fail to liven a game — it kills it. These are the working rules, extracted from what went wrong and right in the founding conversation. Every line of game writing gets audited against them.

## 1. Bits, not premises

A **premise** is "the monster has stage fright." A **bit** is a character with a *want*, a *plan*, and the plan *backfiring in a way that reveals the character*.

Case study (the founding correction): the stage-fright monster was a premise — a quirk wearing a trenchcoat. The fix was Håkan's rewrite: the monster *ordered the mirror maze himself*, out of ambition, to feel surrounded by a hundred monsters — and got destroyed by his own delivery, becoming a narcissist who stares at his reflection all day. Want, hubris, backfire, irony.

**Quirks aren't funny; plans are.** Audit question for every character: what do they want, what did they do about it, and how did that blow up?

- Vexmourne wants the fight to be real → perfects the performance → perfection is the opposite of real.
- Grenadine wants one surprise → builds a rating rubric to force variation → they rehearse *to the rubric*; she critic-proofs herself out of surprise forever.
- Dennis wants to witness history → becomes every role in the show so he can't miss it → when history happens he's *in* it, and forgets his cue.

## 2. The monocle principle

The funniest beat is the one **the player causes**. (Named for Monkey Island's cartographer: stealing his monocle isn't funny on paper — it's funny because *you* did it, in a situation, to a person.) Systemic jokes over scripted ones. The bonk is funnier than any written gag because it happened to *you*, and the game never suggested it.

Corollary: give the player a verb that is always live (the wrench) and never prompted. The space between "I could" and "I did" is where the comedy lives.

## 3. The Wally principle

**The world remembers, and jokes compound.** Consequences resurface across the whole game — letters that find you, NPCs who reappear, a welcome-sign animation that comes back in a church basement. A joke that returns with interest is worth five new ones.

Mechanical version: skipped NPCs remember being skipped. Closed tickets open new tickets. The save file has feelings.

## 4. Play it straight

**One absurdity per land, taken dead seriously by every soul in it.** The economy, religion, infrastructure, and daily manners of the land all flow logically from the single broken premise. Nobody winks. The Debugger is the only person who finds anything strange, and is wrong to.

The Edna & Harvey note: when a fix ruins a life, the game does not point at it. No music sting on the watering can. The player closes the drawer themselves.

## 5. Punch at the industry, never at the players

Including the simulated ones. The inhabitants of genre-lands are idiots you love, like Lemmings. Satire targets: live-service homogenization, crunch, meta-review culture, "games as content." Never: the people who play games, the people who love the wrong games, the people who cry at JRPGs.

## 6. Vocabulary register

Gamer-level, not programmer-level: bugs, lag, escort missions, water levels, day-one patches, speedruns. **No monad jokes.** The test: would a fourteen-year-old who only plays Fortnite laugh? (Wreck-It Ralph proved the theme works at that register.)

## 7. Repetition is where comedy goes to die

- Scripted jokes never gate mechanics.
- No joke may force a second read/listen.
- Running bits must *escalate* on each return, never repeat (the letters get needier; the "Tomorrow's the day" greeting works because it changes meaning, not text).

## 8. The specificity layer

The funny lives in the third detail. Not "an old binder" — a binder held together with tape, annotated in three generations of handwriting: blood, red ink, *gel pen with the anxious little stars of a student who studied very hard.* First drafts stop at the first detail. Punch-up's job is to dig until the detail is so specific it could only belong to this one character.

## 9. Process (how this survives being made at volume)

- **Draft long, cut half.** First-draft AI comedy regresses to "wacky." Volume is the raw material, never the product.
- **The red pen is the engine.** Writers'-room loop: draft → Håkan red-pens → punch-up → kill-rate audit. A sample proves capability, not consistency; only the process proves consistency.
- **Self-flag suspects.** Every script ends with the writer naming the lines most likely to be cut, and the ones they'd defend. (See the appendix of the Penultima script.)
- **The gate rule:** no system gets built until its content is funny on paper. Paper is cheap. Years are not.
