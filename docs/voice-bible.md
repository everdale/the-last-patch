# Voice Bible — so every character doesn't sound the same

The specific risk Håkan named (point 8): with one author — and *especially* with an AI author — voices collapse toward a single house wit. Everyone gets the same rhythm, the same ironic deflation, the same three-beat joke. The cast becomes one clever narrator wearing hats. This document is the guardrail. It is mandatory input before writing any character and an audit gate after.

## Why AI voice-collapse happens (name the enemy)

A language model defaults to *its* funniest register and applies it to everyone. Left unchecked you get: every NPC lands the same dry callback; every line is the same length; every joke is structured want→deadpan→reveal. The fix isn't "try to vary it" (vibes don't scale across contributors). The fix is **per-character constraints that force divergence**, plus a **mechanical test** that fails sameness.

## The Voice Card (fill before writing a line)

Every named character gets one. Six fields, because six independent axes make collisions unlikely:

```
CHARACTER: Dennis
WORLD: Penultima (house style: theatrical / reverent)

1. COMEDIC ENGINE (want → plan → backfire — see humor-doctrine §1)
   Wants to witness history → becomes every role so he can't miss it → when
   history finally happens he is IN it, and forgets his cue.

2. LEXICON
   always: "an honor", "part of it", job-title precision ("second meteor
           technician"), stage directions said aloud
   never:  irony, complaint, profanity, abstraction
   register: earnest, slightly too loud, lists his duties when nervous

3. SENTENCE SIGNATURE
   Short. Declarative. Reverent pauses before the payoff word.
   ("Disbelief. Acceptance. (reverent pause) Clatter.")

4. WHAT THEY'RE WRONG ABOUT
   That his tiny role is the load-bearing one. (It accidentally becomes true.)

5. ESCALATING TIC (the Wally hook — must grow across the game, never repeat flat)
   "It was an honor." → oils the door at 4am, "it was an honor" → weeps onto
   his hero contract → by the letters, signs Vexmourne's dictation and adds
   his own apologetic P.S.

6. NEGATIVE SPACE (what they never joke about)
   The faint. He is dead serious about the faint. Never winks at it.
```

### The six axes, expanded
- **Comedic engine** — the *kind* of funny they are. A character whose engine is "earnest overcommitment" (Dennis) cannot collide with "exhausted connoisseur of boredom" (Grenadine) or "theatrical grief about losing his art" (Vexmourne). Different engines ≠ same jokes.
- **Lexicon** — an always-list and a never-list. The never-list does the heavy lifting: Dennis *never* uses irony; Grenadine is *made* of it. That single rule keeps two characters apart even mid-banter.
- **Sentence signature** — length and rhythm. Vexmourne: landslides and four-hundred-year clauses. Dennis: telegrams. Grenadine: flat, surgical, one twist at the end. The Merchant: salesman-cadence, everything a pitch. You can often tell who's speaking with the words blanked out — that's the goal.
- **What they're wrong about** — every character believes something false, and the gap is their comedy and their humanity. It also keeps them from being a pure joke-dispenser.
- **Escalating tic** — the Wally hook. A returning verbal/behavioural signature that *compounds*. Repetition-without-escalation is the death named in humor-doctrine §7; escalation is the cure.
- **Negative space** — the topic they play 100% straight. Comedy needs a floor of sincerity; the negative space is where the character becomes real and where the Edna-&-Harvey darkness can land.

## World house-styles (so worlds don't feel the same either)

Characters inflect a **per-world register**. Same six-axis individuality, but tuned to a shared dialect, so each genre-land has an audible identity the moment you cross the border:

| World | House style | Sounds like |
|---|---|---|
| **Penultima** (JRPG) | theatrical, reverent, scheduled | everything is a production; grief is about *art* |
| **The Grimdark Kingdom** | mournful, oversharing, purple | every NPC delivers unsolicited tragic backstory |
| **Idle Pastures** | placid, agrarian, zen | pride in stillness; the worst sin is *clicking* |
| **Early Access Swamp** | corporate-optimistic placeholder-speak | "[dialogue coming soon]", load-bearing TODOs |
| **The Open World Plains** | breathless tutorialese | quest-marker brain; can't stop pointing at things |
| **The Metroidvania** | terse, lonely, map-minded | Galahad, free and unbearably alone |

A character = world house-style × their personal Voice Card. Grenadine is "Penultima theatrical" *soured into* exhausted irony — she's of the world and against it, which is why she's the one who can leave.

## The audit gate: the Strip-Names Test

Mandatory before any vignette script is accepted:

> Take any **three** consecutive lines from different characters. Delete the speaker labels. Hand them to someone who knows the cast. **Can they reassign every line correctly?**
> - All three right → voices are distinct. Ship.
> - Any wrong → those two characters have collapsed. Re-tune lexicon/signature/engine until they separate.

Second gate, the **Engine-Collision check**: list the comedic engine of every speaking character in a world. Any two identical? One must change. A world needs contrasting engines the way a band needs more than one instrument.

## Worked contrast (the three Penultima leads, same beat)

The meteor missed, on purpose, for real. Each reacts in their own voice — *no two could be swapped*:

- **Vexmourne** (engine: grief-about-lost-art; landslide sentences): *"I missed. (beat) On purpose. For real."* — the shortest he ever speaks, because it's the truest.
- **Dennis** (engine: earnest overcommitment; telegrams; never ironic): forgets his nine-years-rehearsed faint entirely and just *stands there, blinking* — his comedy is the absence of his tic at the one moment it mattered.
- **Grenadine** (engine: exhausted connoisseur; flat, terminal twist): *"It's not on the rubric."* — and applauds, alone, the only un-bored thing she's done in forty years.

Three sincerities, three engines, three rhythms, one moment. That's the bar.
